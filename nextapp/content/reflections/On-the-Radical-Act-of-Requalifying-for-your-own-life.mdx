This morning, my son turned sixteen months old. I have held him every day of his life, and yet this morning, watching him discover new way of getting down the bed, I wondered if I had earned the right to call myself his father. Not legally. Not by accident of biology. But earned it, the way one might earn the title of craftsman or scholar: through deliberate practice, through failure and return, through the patient accumulation of competence.

The question arrived unbidden, and it unsettled me.

In the corporate world, that strange theatre, where we spend so much of our waking lives—March and April are the months of reckoning. The appraisal season. KPIs materialise on screens like report cards. Targets are set. Incentives dangle. We are asked, in essence, to re-qualify for the roles we already hold. To prove we deserve to continue being whatever title is printed on our business cards.

Most of us endure this with a mixture of resignation and performance. We prepare our accomplishments like offerings. We speak in the language of metrics. We know the dance.

But what struck me this morning, holding my son, was the absence of such accounting in the rest of life. We are never asked to re-qualify as fathers, as sons, as friends. We assume these roles and then—we simply continue. The title persists regardless of performance. We tend to operate as though the role, once assumed, is self-sustaining. A husband can be inattentive for years and remain a husband. A son can forget to call and remain a son. A friend can drift into silence and the word 'friend' still applies when chance brings you together again.

*There are no performance reviews for the human heart.*


## The asymmetry we have normalised

The corporate appraisal is, at its best, an act of honest accounting. You look at what you said you would do, and you ask whether you did it. You look at how you worked with others. You ask what you want from the next year, and what the next year will ask of you.

It is imperfect. It is often political. It is sometimes absurd. The mechanism is imperfect and often politically corrupted, but its underlying logic is sound: positions of responsibility should require ongoing demonstration of fitness.

The strange thing is that we apply it to our work, where the stakes are real but bounded. We do not, most of us, apply it to the places where the stakes are unbounded and irreversible. Consider the asymmetry plainly. To be promoted at work, you must demonstrate competence at regular intervals. Your performance is observed, evaluated, discussed. But, where is the accountability for self? We have built an entire architecture of accountability for our professional lives and almost none for the lives that matter most, the ones conducted not in offices or on quarterly earnings calls, but in kitchens and hospital waiting rooms and the quiet minutes before a child falls asleep.

Imagine if every year, in the way that March brings its corporate reckonings, we had to sit down and answer for ourselves: What kind of father was I this year? What kind of friend? What kind of stranger in the checkout line, in the elevator, in traffic?

Your quarterly numbers can be improved next quarter. The year your child will turn three, the year your mother was ill and you were distracted, the year your friend needed you and you were not quite there, those years do not get a revision.

Because unlike our jobs, these roles do not come with performance reviews built in. We must create them ourselves. We must choose to look clearly at the distance between intention and action. Between the person we tell ourselves we are and the person we have actually been.


## The roles we inherit without interview


Consider all the roles you currently hold. Not the professional ones; those come with job descriptions, with onboarding documents, with someone to tell you what success looks like. I mean the other ones.

Father. Son. Friend. Husband. Brother. The stranger who holds the door open. The neighbour who nods. The person on the other end of the phone when someone is having a bad night. These roles arrive, as the great gifts and the great responsibilities always do, without application forms.

None of these came with an offer letter. You did not negotiate the terms. In most cases, you simply became them gradually, and once you became them, nobody checked in again. Nobody sent you an annual summary of your performance. Nobody asked whether you were still the right person for the role.

I became a father recently. My child is new to the world, still startled by it, still deciding what to make of the light and the sounds and the large, slightly overwhelmed faces peering down at him. I might not felt ready. I am not sure readiness is the right frame. You do not become a parent because you are prepared. You become one because a person arrives, and there you are.

But I have been wondering: what would it mean to re-qualify?


## A structural re-qualification

There is also a structural case for this kind of annual reckoning that has nothing to do with morality and everything to do with the nature of human development. The people in our lives do not remain static. A child at one year is a categorically different creature from a child at four; a child at four bears little resemblance to a child at eleven. A marriage in its second year is navigating entirely different terrain from a marriage in its fifteenth. Parents age. Friends change careers, cities, priorities, beliefs. The role of good son at twenty-three is not the same role as good son at forty-one, when your parents can no longer carry their own luggage.

And yet we often respond to these people with strategies, emotional, relational, communicative, that were calibrated to an earlier version of the situation. We are running outdated software on new hardware, and wondering why the system keeps crashing.

Annual re-qualification is, in part, a software update. A moment to ask: who are these people now, and what does this role require of me in response to who they actually are, rather than who they were when I last seriously thought about it. To face our roles honestly, to ask, year after year, whether we are genuinely inhabiting them or merely occupying them, is to take seriously the people who have trusted us with their need.

## A thought experiment worth sitting with

This is not a performance review. This is something older and harder. This is the work of becoming human. Of earning not the titles we are given, but the right to keep them. Of being worthy, not perfect. Of trying, failing, and trying again.

We are remarkably good at filling our hours. We are considerably less good at examining what those hours have meant to the people who needed something from them. The question is not whether you care. Almost everyone cares. The question is whether your caring has been, over this past year, legible, to the people who needed to be able to read it.

My son' hands open and close in the morning light. He does not know yet that he is teaching me how to be his father. That every day I must re-qualify for the role. That love is not something you are given once and keep forever, but something you must earn again and again, in a thousand small moments, for as long as you are alive. *That is the work. And it has no end date.*

So here is the question I am sitting with this March, and that I would offer to you:
If you had to re-qualify for the roles that matter most if someone asked you to make the case for yourself as the father, the partner, the friend, the human being showing up in the lives of others, what would you put in the application?
