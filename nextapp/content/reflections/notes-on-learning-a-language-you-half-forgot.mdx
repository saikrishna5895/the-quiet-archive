My mother tongue is a language I grew up speaking and then, slowly, over a decade or more of
living elsewhere and reading and thinking in another language, half-forgot. It is not gone. I
can speak it still, but the fluency has the quality of something retrieved rather than inhabited.
The words come, but they come a beat after I need them.

There is a particular embarrassment in this situation. You are not a foreigner to the language, 
which would at least be a clean, defined status. You are something stranger: a person who should
know but doesn't quite. A person who is greeted by native speakers with an expectation of ease
that you cannot entirely meet.

## What getting a language back requires

I have been working at it for the past two years, with varying degrees of consistency. What I have
learned is that reading in a language is different from speaking in it, and speaking is different
from thinking in it, and thinking is where the real work happens and the real work is hardest to
force.

You can study vocabulary. You can conjugate. You can memorise idioms and practise conversations and
read slowly with a dictionary. None of this produces thinking. Thinking in a language requires that
you forget, for a moment, that you are thinking in a language, that the gap between the thought and
the word closes, as it does in your strongest tongue, to the point where the words feel less like
labels and more like the things themselves.

> "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."
> — Ludwig Wittgenstein

## What the language knows that I don't

What has surprised me, in this retrieval project, is how much the language carries that the English
I live in doesn't. Not vocabulary, exactly, though that too, but whole ways of structuring an
experience. The way certain sentences put the feeling before the cause, so you arrive at the emotion
first and the explanation arrives as an afterthought. The way certain relations are named with
a precision that English handles only awkwardly, with descriptive phrases where the other language
has a single word.

I grew up in that structure and then left it. And what I miss, what I am trying to get back is
not just the words but the particular shape of perception that the words trained me into.

## What my son has to do with this

My son is over one now. He is at the uninhibited stage of language acquisition, where the logic of a word is
irrelevant and the sound is everything. He does not know he is learning. He is just using what is
in the air.

I watch him with something close to envy. The words are going in without effort, without anxiety,
without the self-consciousness that makes adult language learning so hard. He will have the language
I am struggling to retrieve, as a mother tongue, without ever having to think about it.

I tell myself this makes my own effort worthwhile. That I am doing it not just for myself but so
that there is something for him to connect to, when he is ready. A language is also a lineage. I
would like to not be the link that breaks it.
