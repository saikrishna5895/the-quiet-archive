# Writing Guidelines

*A note to myself about how to write here.*

---

## The Tone

Write as though you are sending a long letter to a thoughtful friend — someone intelligent, curious, and honest, who has no patience for performance but a great deal of patience for thinking. You do not need to impress this person. You need to be honest with them.

The writers to keep in mind: **Maria Popova** (at The Marginalian) for her ability to connect ideas across centuries and disciplines without sounding contrived, remaining always in service of meaning. **Joan Westenberg** for directness and confessional honesty that never tips into self-indulgence. **Zadie Smith** in her essay mode — intelligent, generous, willing to be uncertain.

---

## What to Write About

Write about things you have actually thought about, not things you think you should think about.

The best essays here will tend to be about:

- **Ideas you cannot let go of.** The ones that keep returning when you are trying to do something else.
- **Experiences that require examination.** The kind you understand better after writing than you did before.
- **Connections you have made.** Between things that don't usually appear in the same sentence. Between the personal and the general.

If you are writing something because it seems like a thing a blog like this should contain, stop. Write something else.

---

## What to Avoid

**Fluff.** Do not pad. Do not use three words where one will do. If a sentence does not earn its place it should not be there.

**The corporate voice.** No *leveraging*, no *pivoting*, no *learnings*. No passive-voiced evasions of responsibility. Write in the first person and mean it.

**Generic advice.** This is not a self-help blog. You are not an authority on how to live. You are a person who is trying to figure out how to live and writing about that process. The difference matters.

**Ironic distance.** You are allowed to find things funny — please find things funny — but not as a way of not meaning them. Sincerity is not naivety.

---

## On Structure

Most good essays have the structure of a thought arriving. They begin somewhere reasonable and end somewhere better. The reader should feel, at the end, that they have travelled.

One idea per essay. Not one topic — one *idea*. The idea may take paragraphs to arrive at. Let it.

Section headings are fine and useful. They are not mandatory. Do not break an essay into sections unless the sections are actually doing different things.

---

## On the Fatherhood Section

Write about your son specifically, not fatherhood in general. Not the lessons of parenthood or the wisdom of children. The specific: his weight when you carry him, the specific object of his current obsession, the exact thing he said that you cannot stop thinking about.

Avoid sentimentality. Sentimentality is emotion that hasn't been earned. The genuine thing — the actual comedy and actual difficulty and actual love — is enough. It does not require amplification.

---

## On Length

Essays here should be long enough to require the reader's real attention and short enough not to abuse it. 800–1,500 words is the right range for most pieces. Some will be shorter, a few longer.

Do not aim for a word count. Aim for the end of the thought.

---

## Before Publishing

Read it aloud. If you stumble, the sentence needs work. If you are bored reading it aloud, the reader will be bored reading it silently.

Ask: *Is this honest?* Not confessional — honest. Not *have I said something private?* but *have I said something true?*

If the answer is yes, publish.
